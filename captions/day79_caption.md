# Day 79 Caption — "Tummo: the inner fire breath" (TUTORIAL)

## TikTok (@stillmeditation)

Tibetan monks have been documented raising their body temperature enough to dry wet sheets in freezing temperatures. The technique is called Tummo. You can learn the basics in under five minutes.

Tummo is an ancient Tibetan breathwork and visualization practice. The word translates roughly to "inner fire." Monks use it to generate heat, enter deep meditative states, and develop what Tibetan Buddhism calls "psychic heat" — a felt sense of internal warmth that transcends the physical. Harvard researcher Herbert Benson documented Tummo practitioners raising peripheral body temperature by up to 17 degrees Fahrenheit in a 1982 study at Rumtek Monastery in Sikkim.

→ Step 1: Find your flame. Sit comfortably and close your eyes. Picture a small flame glowing steadily at your navel. Keep it small and bright — this is your starting point.

→ Step 2: Fuel the fire. Inhale deeply through your nose. As you breathe in, visualize the breath feeding the flame. See it growing brighter, hotter, more intense with every inhale.

→ Step 3: Exhale forcefully. Purse your lips slightly and push the breath out with control. Imagine heat surging outward from your core. Repeat this inhale-exhale cycle five times.

→ Step 4: The vase breath. After the fifth exhale, take one more deep inhale. Hold it. Squeeze your core muscles tight — as if containing a vessel of energy. Hold for as long as comfortable. This is called the vase breath. It is the heart of the Tummo technique.

This is a gateway practice. The breathwork is accessible. The visualization deepens with time. Even one round will produce a noticeable warming sensation if you commit to the imagery.

Your body is the furnace. Tummo is the match.
