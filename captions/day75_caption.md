# Day 75 Caption — "Meditation means detaching from life" (MYTH)

## TikTok (@stillmeditation)

Meditation doesn't pull you away from your life. It pulls you into it.

This is the fear that stops a lot of people from starting. They picture monks in caves, vacant stares, emotional unavailability. They worry that if they meditate long enough, they'll stop caring — about their family, their work, the things that matter. That is not what happens. That is not even close to what happens.

There is a distinction that meditation teachers have been making for centuries. It gets lost in translation. Detachment means walls. Pulling away. Going numb. Non-attachment means open hands. Full engagement — without white-knuckling the outcome. Meditation teaches the second. It does not teach the first.

Here is what the research actually shows. Studies on long-term meditators consistently find higher emotional reactivity to positive stimuli — not lower. They feel joy more vividly. They appreciate ordinary moments more intensely. The dulling effect people fear does not appear in the data.

What does appear: less emotional hijacking. Meditators are less likely to spiral into anxiety, less likely to get snagged on a frustrating moment for hours, less likely to blow up at someone they love because they're still carrying yesterday's argument. That is not numbness. That is regulation.

You participate more — not less. When you're not constantly running simulations about what might go wrong, you're actually present for what's happening. You hear the conversation. You taste the meal. You feel the warmth when someone laughs at something you said. The bandwidth that used to go to background noise goes to actual life.

Clinging is what distorts experience. When you're gripping something — a relationship, an outcome, an identity — you can't see it clearly. You're too close. Non-attachment gives you a half-step back. Not so you disengage. So you can finally see it without the distortion.

You still love deeply. You still feel everything. You just stop confusing the feeling with the need to control what causes it.

That is the whole reframe.
