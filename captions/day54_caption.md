# Day 54 Caption — "How slow breathing rewires your nervous system" (SCIENCE)

## TikTok (@stillmeditation)

There is one number that changes your entire physiology. It is not your heart rate. It is not your VO2 max. It is six.

Six breaths per minute. Five seconds in, five seconds out. That cadence is called the resonance frequency of the cardiovascular system, and it is the rate at which your heart, your lungs, and your vagus nerve stop competing and start cooperating. Save this. You can hit it in five minutes tonight.

→ What actually happens. At six breaths per minute your heart rate stops being a flat baseline and starts riding the breath like a wave. It speeds up gently on the inhale, slows down on the exhale, in a clean rhythmic pattern. This is called respiratory sinus arrhythmia, and it is the single best real-time indicator that your nervous system is regulated and online.

→ Why six. Your cardiovascular system has a built-in resonance frequency, the same way a guitar string has a natural pitch. For almost every adult, that frequency sits between five and seven breaths per minute. Six is close enough for everyone. Breathe at that rate and you create the largest possible swing in heart rate between inhale and exhale.

→ The HRV story. Heart rate variability — HRV — is the size of that swing. High HRV means your vagus nerve is doing its job and you have flexibility between stress and calm. Low HRV means you are stuck in sympathetic dominance. Six breaths per minute drives HRV up faster than almost any other intervention on the market.

→ The science. A 2017 review in Frontiers in Human Neuroscience pooled dozens of studies on slow breathing. The consistent finding: six to ten breaths per minute reduces sympathetic tone, increases parasympathetic tone, lowers blood pressure, improves sustained attention, and shifts brain activity toward the patterns seen in long-term meditators. Same person, different software.

→ The protocol. Sit comfortably. Inhale through the nose for five seconds. Exhale through the nose or mouth for five seconds. No holds. Just smooth waves. Do this for five minutes. Track your HRV overnight if you have a watch or Oura — the lift shows up within a week of daily practice.

This is the most boring breathing technique in the world. It is also the one with the most research behind it. Your nervous system is waiting for the rhythm.

Five minutes. Six breaths per minute. Tonight.
