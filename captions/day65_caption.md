# Day 65 Caption — "Morning vs evening: which practice for which time" (QUICK TIP)

## TikTok (@stillmeditation)

Your morning practice and your evening practice should not be the same thing.

Your nervous system has two modes: activated and deactivated. Breathing and meditation push you in different directions depending on how you use them. Most people pick one technique and run it on autopilot at any hour. That is the thing worth changing.

Morning practice is about activation. You have been calm all night. The goal now is clarity and focus — shifting from rest into engagement. You do not need more stillness. You need ignition.

→ Morning breathwork: box breathing (4-4-4-4) or Kapalabhati. Both sharpen the mind, increase oxygen delivery, and prime the prefrontal cortex for focused work. Three to five minutes before you open your phone.

→ Morning meditation: focused attention on a single anchor — usually the breath. Hold attention firmly. Build the mental grip that carries you through the day. Ten to fifteen minutes.

Evening practice is the opposite direction entirely. Your nervous system has been running the activation circuit since you woke up. The body needs help downshifting. You are not going from tired to more tired — you are going from stimulated to settled.

→ Evening breathwork: 4-7-8 breathing or coherent breathing (inhale 5, exhale 5). Both extend the exhale relative to the inhale, which activates the vagus nerve and drives parasympathetic dominance. Heart rate drops. Cortisol follows.

→ Evening meditation: body scan or loving-kindness. Both are receptive rather than concentrated. You are releasing the residue of the day, not gripping harder at something new.

One practice is not better than the other. They serve different phases of your nervous system's daily rhythm. The mistake is using a waking practice to wind down, or a calming practice when you need to get sharp.

Match the tool to the moment.
