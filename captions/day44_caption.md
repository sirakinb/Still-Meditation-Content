# Day 44 Caption — "The meditation plateau is real — and it's a good sign" (MOTIVATION)

## TikTok (@stillmeditation)

Around week six, meditation gets boring. The early excitement fades. The sessions stop feeling profound. You sit down, do the breathing, and nothing dramatic happens. Most people read this as failure. It is the opposite.

Boredom is the receipt that says your nervous system has adapted. The novelty is gone because your brain is no longer treating the practice as something new. It is becoming wiring. And wiring is the whole point.

→ The plateau is real. Around week six or seven, the dopamine spike from a new habit dries up. Your brain stops releasing the "this is exciting" chemicals because the activity is no longer unfamiliar. The technique still works. Your reward system just stopped clapping for it.

→ This is how skill actually forms. Think about learning to drive. The first month was thrilling and terrifying. By month six, you could drive home and barely remember the trip. That is not regression. That is the skill becoming automatic. Meditation goes through the exact same arc.

→ The trap. Most people quit at the boring middle. They mistake the absence of dramatic feelings for the absence of progress. So they bail right at the moment the practice was starting to actually rewire them. The bliss seekers leave. The builders stay.

→ The shift. Stop chasing peak experiences. Stop grading each session by how it felt. The boring sessions are doing more for your baseline than the exciting ones ever did. Your job is no longer to feel something. Your job is to show up.

→ What is happening underneath. While the surface feels flat, your default mode network is quieting, your prefrontal cortex is strengthening, your stress reactivity is dropping. Brain scans of long-term meditators show these changes accelerating after the initial honeymoon ends.

The boring middle is not the obstacle. It is the practice.

Keep going.
