# Day 74 Caption — "Building your breathwork toolkit: which technique for which need" (QUICK TIP)

## TikTok (@stillmeditation)

Most people use one breathing pattern for everything. That is like using a hammer for every job in the toolbox.

Your breath has seven distinct gears. Each one targets a different state in your nervous system. The key is knowing which tool to reach for and when.

→ Stress. 4-7-8 breathing. Inhale for 4 counts. Hold for 7. Exhale for 8. The extended exhale activates your parasympathetic nervous system faster than almost any other technique. Alternatively, coherent breathing — inhale 5, exhale 5 — for 5 minutes. Both drop cortisol within minutes.

→ Focus. Box breathing. 4 counts in. 4 hold. 4 out. 4 hold. This is the pattern Navy SEALs use before high-stakes operations. The equal-ratio pattern stabilizes your heart rate and brings your prefrontal cortex fully online.

→ Energy. Kapalabhati or Wim Hof. Kapalabhati: 30 rapid forceful exhales, then a full exhale and breath hold. Wim Hof: 30 deep power inhales, full exhale, hold. Both flood your bloodstream with oxygen and adrenaline. Use these instead of a third coffee.

→ Sleep. 4-7-8 again. The 7-count hold and long exhale shift your nervous system from alert to rest in under two minutes. Do it lying down. Do not skip the hold.

→ Emotional regulation. 2:1 exhale ratio. Inhale for 4. Exhale for 8. When emotions spike, the vagus nerve responds to a long slow exhale. Doubling your exhale is the fastest way to shift your body out of a stress loop.

→ Balance. Alternate nostril breathing. Block the right nostril and inhale through the left. Switch. Exhale through the right. Repeat. Five minutes of this syncs your left and right brain hemispheres. It is the most calming technique in the Ayurvedic toolkit.

→ Pre-meditation. Cyclic sighing. Two inhales through the nose — the second one tops off your lungs — then one long slow exhale through the mouth. Three cycles of this before you sit down to meditate drops baseline tension by 30 percent before the session even starts.

Seven tools. Seven states. One breath.
