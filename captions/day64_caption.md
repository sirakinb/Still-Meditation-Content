# Day 64 Caption — "Bhastrika: the bellows breath" (TUTORIAL)

## TikTok (@stillmeditation)

Kapalabhati is the gateway. Bhastrika is what's on the other side.

Most people learn Kapalabhati and think they've found the most powerful breathwork available. They haven't. Bhastrika — the bellows breath — takes the same principle and doubles it. Equal force. Full volume. Both directions.

Here is what makes it different.

Kapalabhati is passive on the inhale — you let the breath rush in as a reflex after the sharp exhale. Bhastrika removes that asymmetry. Every inhale is as forceful as every exhale. You are fully engaged on both sides of the breath. The diaphragm is working like a bellows — hence the name.

→ The breath. Inhale sharply and fully through the nose. Belly expands completely. Then exhale with the same force — push every bit of air out. No passive coasting. Equal power, both ways.

→ The round. 20 rapid, vigorous breaths equal one round. Then stop completely. Breathe normally. Let your nervous system absorb what just happened. Repeat for 3 rounds total, resting 30 to 60 seconds between each.

→ Why it's more intense. Kapalabhati is activating. Bhastrika is energizing on a different level — it floods the body with oxygen, rapidly activates the sympathetic system, then swings into deep calm during the rest phase. Athletes use it pre-performance. Meditators use it to cut through mental fog in minutes.

→ The prerequisite. Do not start here. Bhastrika demands full diaphragm engagement, breath control, and the ability to feel when your body is nearing its limit. If you feel dizzy, stop immediately. This is a graduation from Kapalabhati — not a replacement for it, not a shortcut around it.

When done right, your body hums. Your mind clears. Your energy sharpens.

This is what 3 minutes of focused breathing can do.
